The 2026 guide to Chinese tourist visas for U.S. passport holders

What's changed, what's stayed the same, and the fastest path through the consulate in San Francisco. Plus when to use the 240-hour visa-free transit instead.

The short version

As of early 2026, Americans visiting China for tourism still need a visa — the blanket visa-free entry that applies to many European and Southeast Asian countries has not been extended to U.S. passport holders. However, China upgraded its transit exemption in December 2024, and the new 240-hour (10-day) visa-free transit policy is genuinely useful for a trip like ours. Whether it applies depends on your itinerary — and for most participants, it likely does.

The 240-hour visa-free transit

Since December 2024, China's transit exemption runs 240 hours — that's 10 full days, upgraded from the previous 144-hour (6-day) limit. The clock starts at 00:00 the day after you enter mainland China, which in practice gives you comfortable room for a 10-day itinerary.

To qualify, you need to enter China via a participating port (Shanghai Pudong is one), have a confirmed onward ticket out of China before the 240 hours expire, and be transiting between two different regions. That last point is the key one: your origin and your onward destination must be different countries or regions — but Hong Kong counts as a separate region from mainland China for this purpose.

This means a routing of US → Hong Kong → mainland China → US can qualify. You fly into Hong Kong first, cross by land into Shenzhen, travel through the mainland, and fly home from Shanghai. Because your "before" region (Hong Kong) and your onward destination (the US) are different, the transit exemption applies. The old assumption — that starting in Hong Kong disqualified you — was based on the previous rules and is no longer accurate.

For our Discover China's Tech itinerary

Good news: most participants on our tour should be able to use the 240-hour transit exemption rather than applying for a full tourist visa. That saves you the consulate visit, the $185 fee, and several weeks of lead time.

There is one important booking condition to keep in mind: your return flight from Shanghai should go directly to the US, without a stopover in Hong Kong. A layover back through Hong Kong on the return can disqualify the transit exemption, since it complicates the "transiting between two different regions" requirement. Book a nonstop or one-stop routing that avoids Hong Kong on the way home and you should be fine.

We flag this not to complicate your booking — nonstop flights from Shanghai (PVG) to SFO, LAX, and JFK are plentiful — but because it's the one itinerary detail worth checking before you lock in your flights.

A note on eligibility

Transit rules involve some interpretation, and border officers apply them with discretion. We're confident the 240-hour exemption works for the routing described above — we've seen it applied successfully — but we can't guarantee your individual outcome at immigration. If you want a second set of eyes on your specific flights before booking, reach out and we're happy to think it through with you. And if you'd rather just have the certainty of a visa in your passport, the standard tourist visa is always a solid option (see below).

Getting a tourist visa through San Francisco

The Chinese Consulate in San Francisco serves applicants in California, Oregon, Washington, Nevada, Arizona, and Hawaii. The process is straightforward but paper-heavy. Here's what to expect:

  • Application form: Fill out the Chinese Visa Application Form (Form V.2013) online at the COVA system and print it. Double-check every field — errors get flagged and slow things down.
  • Passport: Must be valid for at least six months beyond your intended return date, with at least two blank pages.
  • Photo: One recent passport-style photo (2"×2"), white background. Many applicants get rejected for photos that don't meet the spec — use a dedicated photo booth or a photo service.
  • Flight itinerary: A confirmed round-trip or onward flight booking. It doesn't have to be fully paid — most airlines will issue an itinerary confirmation without a full ticket.
  • Hotel bookings: Confirmations for your first and last nights at minimum. A printed itinerary covering the whole trip is cleaner.

Processing times and fees

Standard processing at the San Francisco consulate runs 4 business days. Rush service (2 business days) and same-day service are available for additional fees — roughly $30 and $50 extra respectively, on top of the standard $185 fee for U.S. applicants. Appointment slots fill up fast in spring and fall; book at least three to four weeks before your travel date.

The consulate also works with visa agencies like CITS and VisaHQ if you'd rather not go in person. Expect to add $50–$100 in agency fees, plus a few extra days for mail handling.

A few things that trip people up

The consulate is strict about the application form matching your passport exactly — middle names, hyphenated surnames, and address formatting have all caused delays. If your passport has a "see page X" notation for a middle name, list it. If you have a prior visit to Taiwan in your passport, note it honestly; it won't disqualify you but omitting it causes problems.

Plan to apply at least six weeks before departure if you can. Peak demand runs March through October, and appointment availability tightens considerably.
